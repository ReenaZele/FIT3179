GLOBAL DIETS

Food has the potential to nurture and support human health, however for many it serves the opposite. It is a massive challenge to feed a rapidly growing population, where 800 million people lack sufficient food and many more do not have access to a high quality diet. With rising challenges such as these, it is vital to understand

diet patterns across different regions and health issues that correlate to them. To disseminate the importance of diet, this tool provides a series of visualisations that depict diet composition across the world, the impact of wealth on diet and a diet's implications on health.

What is the recommended diet?

In 2019, the EAT-Lancet commission on healthy diets released a report describing the composition of a scientifically supported healthy 2500 calorie adult diet. Their target diet was divided into plant protein, animal protein, carbohydrates as well as fats and sugars. Although the ideal diet varies from person to person, they aim to quantify a universal healthy diet that is also environmentally sustainable. What do different countries eat?

A further diet breakdown by country is provided on the right to show changes over time. It is common to observe an increase in fat and oil consumption, as well as processed meat as these products become more popular and accessible over time.

Does a country's wealth effect the diet of its population?

It is evident below that there are clear divisions in diet depending on a country's wealth. Those that have more wealth have access to more expensive commodities, including meat-based proteins whereas economically poor countries rely more on sustainable grains for dietary supplementation.

Diet and Its Impact on Global Health

Unhealthy diets due to micronutrient deficiencies contribute to a greater risk of morbidity and mortality than unsafe sex, alcohol and drug and tobacco use combined. Increased production of processed foods is coupled with its rising popularity due to changing lifestyles and rapid urbanisation.

There is global effort to improve diet, where WHO member states are aiming to reduce the population's salt intake by 30% by 2025 due to its contribution to diabetes and obesity.

Obesity is considered a rapidly rising pandemic, where alarmingly 1.9 billion adults worldwide were considered overweight with 650 million of those considered obese in 2016. A body mass index (BMI) over 25 is considered overweight, and a BMI over 30 is considered obese.

The choropleth map below demonstrates the distribution of obesity and overweightness across the world, over time. At the moment, more people are obese than underweight in every region except sub-Saharan Africa and Asia. Interestingly, the regions with the highest levels of obesity are located in small Pacific Island States due to a high amount of imported foods high in salt and fat.
