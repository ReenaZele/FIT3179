🥪 GLOBAL DIETS 🥗

Food has the potential to nurture and support human health, however for many it serves the opposite. It is a massive challenge to feed a rapidly growing population, where 800 million people lack sufficient food and many more do not have access to a high quality diet. With rising challenges such as these, it is vital to understand diet patterns across different regions and health issues that correlate to them.

To disseminate the importance of diet, this tool provides a series of visualisations that depict diet composition across the world, the impact of wealth on diet and a diet's implications on health.

What is the recommended diet?

In 2019, the EAT-Lancet commission on healthy diets released a report describing the composition of a scientifically supported healthy 2500 calorie adult diet. Their target diet was divided into plant protein, animal protein, carbohydrates as well as fats and sugars. Although the ideal diet varies from person to person, they aim to quantify a universal healthy diet that is also environmentally sustainable.

Diet and Its Impact on Global Health

Unhealthy diets due to micronutrient deficiencies contribute to a greater risk of morbidity and mortality than unsafe sex, alcohol and drug and tobacco use combined. Increased production of processed foods is coupled with its rising popularity due to changing lifestyles and rapid urbanisation.

There is global effort to improve diet, where WHO member states are aiming to reduce the population's salt intake by 30% by 2025 due to its contribution to diabetes and obesity.

Obesity is considered a rapidly rising pandemic, where alarmingly 1.9 billion adults worldwide were considered overweight with 650 million of those considered obese in 2016. A body mass index (BMI) over 25 is considered overweight, and a BMI over 30 is considered obese.

The choropleth map below demonstrates the distribution of obesity and overweightness, across the world, over time. At the moment, more people are obese than underweight in every region except sub-Saharan Africa and Asia. Interestingly, the regions with the highest levels of obesity are located in small Pacific Island States due to a high amount of imported foods high in salt and fat.

Diseases Caused by Poor Diet

Several dietary deficits are directly correlated to increased risks of developing several non-communicable diseases. Across all considered factors, heart disease as a cause of poor diet is most prevalent.

Evidence suggests that this is due to the dietary connection between blood pressure, body ion balance and circulatory health. For example, processed meat and red meat contain high fat which can accumulate in one's arteries and/or veins causing heart disease or stroke. Vegetables, fruits, legumes, nuts and seeds all work in the opposite, helping to prevent heart disease, stroke and other disease.
